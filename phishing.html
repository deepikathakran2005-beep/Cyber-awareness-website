1. What is Phishing?

Phishing is a social-engineering attack where an attacker pretends to be a trusted person or company to trick you into giving sensitive information (passwords, bank details, OTPs) or downloading malware. Phishing can arrive via email, SMS, messaging apps, social media, or even phone calls (called vishing).

Tip: Always verify unexpected requests for credentials or payments.

2. History & Evolution

1990s: AOL Phishing

Attackers sent fake AOL login pages to steal credentials from users. Early attacks were unsophisticated, relying on mass emails and basic deception.

2000s: Targeted Banking Attacks

Attackers started targeting bank users with personalized emails. Email spoofing became widespread.

2010s: Spear Phishing and Whaling

Highly targeted attacks focusing on executives (whaling) or specific individuals (spear phishing) became common.

Today: Multi-Channel Attacks

Modern phishing attacks use email, SMS (smishing), voice calls (vishing), social media, and fake websites to trick victims.

3. Types of Phishing

  • Email Phishing: Mass emails pretending to be from banks, services, or trusted organizations.
  • Spear Phishing: Highly personalized attacks using the target's name, role, or interests.
  • Whaling: Targeting high-profile individuals like CEOs or managers.
  • Smishing: Phishing via SMS messages.
  • Vishing: Voice calls impersonating trusted authorities.
  • Clone Phishing: Replicating legitimate emails with malicious attachments or links.
  • Website Phishing: Fake websites mimicking legitimate sites to harvest credentials.
  • Pharming: Redirecting users to fake websites even when they type the correct URL.

4. Red Flags to Detect Phishing

  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer".
  • Urgent or threatening language.
  • Unexpected attachments or links.
  • Slightly misspelled URLs.
  • Requests for sensitive info (passwords, OTPs, bank info).
  • Too-good-to-be-true offers or prize notifications.
  • Poor grammar and spelling mistakes in official-looking emails.

5. Real-World Case Studies

PayPal Attack (2015)

Users received emails about unauthorized transactions. Clicking the link led to credential theft.

Microsoft 365 Corporate Phishing (2020)

Attackers targeted employees with fake login pages to steal corporate credentials.

COVID-19 Relief Scams (2020)

Attackers exploited relief fund emails to commit identity theft and bank fraud.

6. Attack Techniques

  • Link Manipulation: URLs appear correct but redirect elsewhere.
  • Website Spoofing: Fake login pages mimicking official sites.
  • Malware Attachments: Embedded malware in attachments (trojans, ransomware).
  • Social Engineering: Creating urgency, fear, or curiosity to trick users.
  • Credential Harvesting Forms: Fake forms that collect username and password.

7. Prevention & Protection

  • Verify sender emails and URLs before clicking.
  • Hover over links to see actual URL.
  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
  • Use strong, unique passwords and password managers.
  • Keep devices, apps, and browsers updated.
  • Educate yourself and colleagues about phishing techniques.
  • Use browser/email anti-phishing tools.

8. Incident Response & Reporting

  1. Do not click links or open attachments.
  2. Inform your IT/security team immediately.
  3. Forward phishing emails to authorities (CERT-In, local cybercrime units).
  4. Change compromised passwords and enable MFA.
  5. Scan your system for malware.
  6. Document incident for lessons learned and awareness training.
